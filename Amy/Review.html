Cirque du Soleil 60-minute special: three unique shows of mechanics, of water, and of the wild Mexico all in one!

By Amy Zhang
Published February 27th @ 10:23pm

KURIOS - Cabinet of Curiosities

As the scene emerged from the dark, our view shifted to the old-fashioned laboratory, the curio cabinet, of “an ambitious inventor who defies the laws of time, space and dimension”. With a work of 'magic', small globes of light illuminated inside a glass jar while the ticking of the clock chimed in the background. As the number on the clock reached 11:11, the ticking sped up and the entire laboratory drifted away. With a final buildup of music, actors lowered gracefully, as if from the end of time, behind white clouds of air from the ceiling as the music reached the climax. This was fascinating beginning the 60-minute special #1 Cirque du Soleil had performed, composed of parts from KURIOS, “O”, and LUZIA.

This Cirque du Soleil performance has, in general, a feeling of mysteriousness but also a sense of wildness. The mystical feeling rooted from the gears, the antique-looking KURIOS stage and costumes, and the baffling storyline. You would see men and women wearing cowboy clothing, juggling and dancing, as if gambling in a typical Old-West setting. In the background, you would hear bright staccato piano notes while artists danced and smiled wholeheartedly. Or, instead, you may hear dramatic music that creates a suspenseful atmosphere as the juggler performs an unbelievable trick. All of these well designed soundtracks had made the performance ever so interesting. Soon, the scene transitioned. One beam of glistening light shone on a sweet smiling woman and followed her as she rolled a boat on the outskirts of the stage in darkness, until she faded into a white mist. This type of lighting was used to emphasize the woman in order to make time for the other artists to transition.

The wildness came from “O” and LUZIA, which, combined, resemble somewhat a jungle. For example, in “O”, a huge water pool was at the heart of the stage, with layers of thin curtains at the back, barely revealing the shadows of various human figures. About a dozen of performers were on a floating platform on top of the pool, performing aquatic acrobatics and complicated, elegant movements. The artists' costumes and makeup were what made them look inhuman and resemble mysterious wild animals. Their faces were colored with makeup the same color of their costume -- light green, magenta, and neon yellow. Their bodies were covered with stripes of colors but mostly skin color. These were what made audiences feel as if they were elves in a primitive, hidden world instead of the theater of a civilized world.

Overall, I rate this performance four and a half stars out of five. I highly recommend this free exciting opportunity to experience the fascinating world created by Cirque du Soleil. Despite that the story line was unclear, if any had even existed, this viewing experience would be absolutely enjoyable and astounding with much to appreciate.
